PART 4: Niche vs. Broad — The Strategic Answer

Your question: Should I niche down to a specific type of manufacturer, or stay broad across all manufacturers that need website improvements? Here is the honest, research-backed answer.

4.1 — The Case for Staying Broad (Your Income Argument Is Correct)
The conventional agency wisdom is 'niche down as much as possible.' And that wisdom is right — eventually. But you are at Stage 1, not Stage 3. Here is why broad is the right call right now:

	•	The RGV alone has 300+ manufacturers across 15+ sub-sectors — you need volume to build case studies, referrals, and revenue before you can afford to narrow
	•	Your 16-year manufacturing background already IS your niche — 'web design for manufacturers' is already a vertical niche compared to every generalist agency in your market
	•	Manufacturing websites share 90% of the same core problems regardless of sub-sector: no capabilities page, no RFQ form, poor SEO, no professional photography. Your solution set doesn't change much between a plastic molder and a metal fabricator
	•	Income precedes positioning — you need 10-15 client case studies before your portfolio has enough depth to narrow without losing prospects
	•	Texas has 19,000+ manufacturing establishments. Even capturing 0.1% of that market is 190 clients at $4,000 average = $760,000 in revenue

4.2 — The Evolutionary Path (Broad Now, Strategic Later)
Here is the three-stage evolution that mirrors how the most successful niche agencies built their businesses:

STAGE
TIMEFRAME
POSITIONING & FOCUS
Stage 1: INCOME
Months 1-12
All manufacturers in RGV and Texas. Build 10-15 case studies. Build referral network inside STMA. Generate $60,000-$80,000 first-year revenue.
Stage 2: AUTHORITY
Months 12-24
Identify your 2-3 strongest sub-sectors from portfolio data. Begin targeting those verticals specifically. Add industry-specific landing pages. Start speaking at STMA events.
Stage 3: DOMINANCE
Year 2-3+
Own 1-2 specific manufacturing verticals nationally (e.g., 'the web studio for Texas metal fabricators'). Command $8,000-$15,000 project pricing. Referral-driven growth.

BOTTOM LINE ON NICHE: You are already niched by being manufacturer-only. That is your moat. Stay broad WITHIN manufacturing for now. Let your portfolio tell you which sub-sectors love you most — then double down on those. Income first. Precision later.

5.6 — Key Design Principles for Every Manufacturer Site You Build

	•	SPEED FIRST: Target PageSpeed score of 85+ on mobile. Compress all images. Use clean template. This is non-negotiable.
	•	RFQ FORM ABOVE THE FOLD: The quote request form or button must be visible without scrolling on every device.
	•	CERTIFICATIONS PROMINENT: ISO, AS9100, IATF, NADCAP badges go in the header or hero — not buried in the footer.
	•	CAPABILITIES PAGE AS CORE NAV: This is the most-searched page by procurement. It goes in primary navigation, not hidden in About.
	•	REAL PHOTOGRAPHY ONLY: No stock photos of people shaking hands. Shop floor, equipment, finished parts, team members.
	•	INDUSTRIES SERVED = TRUST SIGNAL: A dedicated section or page showing which industries you've served (Aerospace, Automotive, Medical, Defense) immediately qualifies the manufacturer as a serious supplier.
	•	MOBILE FIRST: Design for the procurement manager reading specs on their phone between meetings. If it doesn't work on mobile, it doesn't work.
	•	COPY WRITTEN FOR ENGINEERS: Specific tolerances, materials, processes, and certifications — not vague quality statements.
	•	NO GATED SPEC SHEETS: Procurement teams abandon sites that require contact info before downloading a PDF. Give specs freely; collect contact info through the RFQ.
	•	CLEAR NEXT STEP: Every page ends with one CTA. Either 'Request a Quote' or 'View Our Capabilities.' Never more than one.
